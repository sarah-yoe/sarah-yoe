Literature Review: Why Academic Work Should Be Published as HTML — and Why the Web Was Built for It

Academic communication has traditionally relied on formats like PDF and print, but a strong case is emerging for publishing scholarly work as HTML on the World Wide Web. The web itself was originally conceived as a solution for researchers to share information freely and easily through interconnected hypertext documents. Tim Berners-Lee (1989) proposed a universal, linked information system for the scientific community, establishing the conceptual foundation of the Web. He later articulated a defining principle of this vision: “The power of the Web is in its universality. Access by everyone regardless of disability is an essential aspect” (WAI, 2024). This literature review examines why the web’s underlying design principles make HTML an ideal format for academic work.

Accessibility and Universality of Content

A core principle of the World Wide Web is that it should work for all people, regardless of hardware, software, language, location, or ability (WAI, 2024). Web accessibility standards WAI (2024) emphasize designing content so that people with disabilities can perceive and navigate it, which in turn benefits everyone in various contexts such as mobile-phone or low-bandwidth users. HTML is inherently suited for accessibility because it supports semantic structure such as headings and alt text for images that assistive technologies can interpret. Publishing research in HTML directly aligns with these ideals: it ensures research findings can be accessed by the widest audience, including people with disabilities and those using diverse devices. Academic knowledge disseminated as accessible web content thus upholds the web’s universality and the principle that the web is “for all people” including those with visual, auditory, cognitive, or motor impairments (W3C, 2021). By contrast, academic PDFs often present barriers because text might not reflow on small screens or images might lack alternative text, which makes them challenging for screen readers and mobile devices.

Hypertext, Linking, and the Web’s Architecture

HTML stands for HyperText Markup Language, with hypertext being the defining feature of the web. The web’s architecture was built around the concept of interconnected documents identified by URLs, which allow anyone to link resources together into a “remarkable information space of interrelated resources” spanning all languages and media (W3C Technical Architecture Group, 2004). Hyperlinks are not just a technical convenience; they are fundamental to scholarly work, where referencing and building upon prior knowledge is key. The Architecture of the World Wide Web notes that a format should support embedded links; otherwise it creates dead-ends: “Good practice: Hypertext links… Data formats that do not allow content authors to create hypertext links lead to the creation of ‘terminal nodes’ on the Web.” (W3C Technical Architecture Group, 2004). PDF documents, while they can contain clickable links, are often treated as self-contained endpoints – “terminal nodes” that are not seamlessly integrated into the live web of content. In contrast, an HTML scholarly article is part of the live web: its references can be clickable hyperlinks to sources or datasets, and it can itself be indexed, linked to, and accessed directly via a URL. This structure supports what Berners-Lee called a “web of documents” enabling researchers to connect citations, datasets, and context in a dynamic way (Berners-Lee, 2006). Using HTML for academic work thus takes full advantage of the web’s hypertext architecture – enabling any reader to immediately navigate from a citation to the cited source, or from an article to related materials, thereby enriching the research dialogue. By publishing in HTML, academics ensure their work participates in the interconnected scholarly conversation rather than remaining a siloed document.

Open Standards and Interoperability

Underpinning the success of the web is a commitment to open standards and interoperability. HTML, CSS and JavaScript, the core web technologies, are open and royalty-free, meaning anyone can implement or use them (W3C, 2021). The OpenStand (2012) principles for modern standards highlight “availability of standards to all” and “voluntary adoption”, along with transparency and consensus in their development (IETF Chair, 2012). In practical terms, an academic article published as HTML adheres to an open standard that any web browser, tool, or user can access without specialized software or paywalls. It is not locked into a proprietary format or a single vendor’s ecosystem. Indeed, the W3C website emphasizes that data on the Web should not be “locked in to particular systems or be only readable by the applications which created it. Users should be free to switch between applications and share data between them” a point also cited in the agency slides from The Socially Aware Web course (W3C, 2017, Capadisli, 2025). A PDF often requires a specific reader or plugin and can have poor interoperability, for example because data or figures inside PDFs cannot be easily reused or extracted. HTML content, on the other hand, can be consumed by web browsers, indexed by search engines, repurposed with ease, for example by converting it to other formats or text-mining it, and remains accessible as technologies evolve. Open standards also future-proof scholarly knowledge: well-formed HTML is likely to remain renderable for decades thanks to the web community’s commitment to backward compatibility and archiving, whereas obscure or proprietary formats risk obsolescence. By publishing academic work in HTML, scholars leverage the web’s open, interoperable foundation, ensuring their work is maximally available and usable, embodying the idea that open access to information empowers knowledge for all (Oever & Cath, 2017).

End-User Focus and Ethical Considerations

The literature also highlights that web technologies and content should be designed with a user-first mindset. The IETF’s “The Internet is for End Users” (RFC 8890) and W3C’s “Priority of Constituencies” principle assert that when making design or publishing decisions, the needs of end users should come before those of authors, implementers, or platforms (Nottingham, 2020, Members of the TAG, 2025). Applying this ethic to academic publishing: the paramount goal is to serve the reader’s experience and access. An HTML publication offers a reader-responsive experience, as content can adapt to different screen sizes, users can adjust text size or styles, and assistive technologies can better interpret structured HTML. It also allows interactive elements such as embedded multimedia or data visualizations that can aid understanding. These features clearly benefit the end user. A static PDF, by contrast, often offers a one-size-fits-all layout with fixed pagination designed for printing, which on a small screen or for a visually impaired reader is far from ideal. Ethical web principles state, “the web should empower an equitable, informed and interconnected society” enabling knowledge-sharing for everyone (W3C, 2021, W3C, 2024). Publishing academic work as HTML directly supports an equitable knowledge ecosystem: it makes information available to anyone with web access, not just those with access to specific journals or software. Scientific and scholarly information, when freely accessible on the open web, empowers education, innovation, and informed citizenship globally.

Conclusion

In conclusion, the reviewed materials collectively argue that publishing academic work as HTML is not merely a technical choice, but a choice that upholds the very values the Web was built upon. It maximizes accessibility and inclusion (WAI, 2024, W3C, 2021), fully leverages hypertext linking to integrate scholarship into the global knowledge network (W3C Technical Architecture Group, 2004), relies on open standards to ensure interoperability and longevity (IETF Chair, 2012), and prioritizes the experience and rights of end users (Members of the TAG, 2025).


 

Reflective Essay: Imagining an Academic Future in HTML

I still remember the first time I tried to read a dense academic PDF on my phone. I pinched, zoomed, scrolled horizontally by accident, lost my place, and eventually gave up. The document felt like a caged animal, alive with ideas yet trapped inside an unyielding frame designed for A4 paper. At the time, I did not question it. PDFs were “how academia works”. End of story.

Then came The Socially Aware Web course—and the story changed.

The Course That Shifted My Perspective

From the very first lecture it became clear that the Web was never meant to be a glorified digital filing cabinet. Seeing the evolution of media from cave paintings to the first hypertext systems made me realize something surprising: every major leap in communication came from a shift in the medium, not just the message. McLuhan’s “medium is the message” hit differently when I saw the Web described as a social machine (McLuhan et al., 1967). It dawned on me that academic PDFs are relics of the printing age awkwardly transplanted into a digital environment. Meanwhile, the Web, with its open standards and universal access, is a thriving ecosystem waiting for academia to catch up.

My Frustration with PDFs (and Proprietary Tools)

I began thinking about my own experiences:

·         Downloading a 50 MB PDF just to read a 3-page methods section.

·         Searching within a document only to find that the text was actually an image.

·         Trying to copy a paragraph and getting broken line breaks and numbering.

·         Struggling to navigate using a screen reader in a volunteer accessibility project.

Every one of those frustrations mapped directly onto a problem the course materials discussed: lack of interoperability, accessibility failures, and reliance on proprietary ecosystems. PDFs freeze content in place. They privilege layout over meaning. They enforce a visual rigidity that’s completely at odds with the Web’s principles.

Experiencing HTML as a Living Document

During the course, when I opened The Socially Aware Web course slides in HTML, something clicked. They were not just slides, they were documents built to be explored. I could link to specific sections, access embedded videos, view images with alternative text, and follow standards references in context. HTML wasn’t a format. HTML was a space. As I explored this idea further, I started imagining what academic life would look like if our papers were designed in the same way, with interaktive graphs instead of static screenshots, fine-grained annotations à la dokieli, machine-readable metadata integrated directly into the document, and live references that update automatically when new versions become available (Capadisli, n.d.). Even readers would benefit from being able to switch languages at the interface level, thanks to built-in internationalization support. Suddenly, the idea of submitting work as HTML did not feel like rebellion – it felt like realism.

Accessibility as a Moral Imperative

One lecture moment stayed with me: the reminder that “the Web is for all people”. That sentence took on even greater significance when I began thinking about fictive individuals during the course. Let us imagine two students: Alex, who has a screen reader due to a visual impairment, and Lea, who accesses the internet via a slow connection in a rural region. Both are passionate learners, eager to read cutting-edge research. A traditional academic article delivered as a PDF can be a dead-end for them. It may be inaccessible, heavy to download, or poorly structured. But an HTML version? It’s lightweight, responsive, semantically rich. It works with Alex’s screen reader, adapting to Lea’s device. It just works. The Web was built for these users. The W3C’s accessibility principles are not just checklists; they are an ethos: design so everyone can participate. In HTML, this ethos is real through alt text, proper headings, landmarks, semantic structure, language tags, alt text, ARIA roles and responsive layouts. When academic content is presented this way, it says: You matter. Your access matters.

Reflecting on this made something crystal clear:

If we choose a format that excludes people, we are responsible for the exclusion.

Neglecting accessibility is not a neutral act – it is an academic version of building stairs where a ramp should be.

The Ethical Dimension: Control, Centralization, and Agency

The course delved deep into governance, centralization risks, and the erosion of user agency. It made me realize that academic publishing’s dependence on PDFs and proprietary platforms is not just inconvenient – it is political.

When a paper lives as a PDF:

  • It is locked into a single representation.

·         It often sits behind paywalls or platform restrictions.

·         Its structure is opaque to search engines and assistive tech.

·         It cannot participate fully in linked data ecosystems.

·         It reinforces power structures in academic publishing.

HTML, by contrast, supports self-publishing and decentralization. It empowers scholars to own their work, host it on their own domains, and contribute to the global knowledge commons.

I found myself imagining a world where every researcher maintains a small “digital garden” of their papers – interlinked, annotated, remixable, queryable. A world where academic publishing looks less like a warehouse and more like a vibrant ecosystem.

Imagining a Scientific Future in HTML

Let me paint the picture I now carry with me:

You publish your research on your own website as an HTML document. It includes a live dataset, interactive visualizations, and annotations from colleagues. A student halfway across the world reads it on their phone, perfectly rendered. They highlight a paragraph and add a public annotation via a Web-standard protocol.

A week later, someone in another country forked your dataset, added new entries, and linked your analysis to theirs using RDF. A search engine indexes both the document and the data structure, making it discoverable by topic and methodology – not just keywords.

Your work does not sit in a drawer. It lives on the Web, woven into the fabric of global knowledge.

This is not science fiction. The technologies already exist. What is missing is cultural adoption.

From Reflection to Commitment

By the end of the course, I found myself returning again and again to one idea:

Choosing HTML is choosing the future of scholarship.

It is a decision grounded in:

·         accessibility

·         transparency

·         interoperability

·         ethics

·         sustainability

·         decentralization

·         durability

It aligns with the Web’s design and with academic values: openness, verifiability, and shared progress.

Publishing as HTML no longer feels like an eccentric experiment. It feels like a responsibility.

Conclusion: A Personal Turning Point

Writing this reflection in HTML (as I intend to submit it) feels like participating, however modestly, in that future. For the first time, my academic work is not confined to a static page – it is living in the medium it was meant for.

The course gave me more than technical knowledge. It gave me a new narrative: that the Web was built for documents like ours – and that academia, when it embraces this medium, becomes more inclusive, more ethical, and more alive.

Maybe someday we will look back at PDFs the way we look at the printing press on The Socially Aware Web course slides – an important chapter in history, but no longer the main story.

And maybe, in that future, our scholarship will finally feel at home.
